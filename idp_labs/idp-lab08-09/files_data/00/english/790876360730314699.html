How to Help an Autistic Family Member Cope with Moving House

18 Nov 2019

Moving house is a difficult process for anyone. It can be especially stressful for an autistic person, and you may worry about how to help manage their feelings and keep them safe during a move. Preparation and consideration can help reduce stress and make the moving experience as easy as possible.

Method 1 - Making Emotional Preparations

1. Give them enough notice. You want to give your loved one time to mentally prepare for the move.[1] Younger children may need a few weeks, while older kids, teens, or adults should get at least a month of warning.

 

2. Explain why the family is moving. Give an age-appropriate description of the reasons.[2]

  • "We're moving to be close to a good school."
  • "Mommy got a new job that will be more fun for her and give us more money. We're moving to be close to her work."
  • "We need a bigger house so you can have your own room and the baby can have his own room."
  • "We are moving so I can be closer to work. I will spend less time in the car. I'll have more free time, and maybe I can spend more time with you."

 

3. Explain what will stay the same. Knowing that some things won't change can be reassuring, especially to small children who may not understand.

  • "You will go to the same school."
  • "All our things will come with us. We'll have the same furniture. You'll have the same toys."
  • "We will still visit Grandma and Uncle Rashid. In fact, it will be easier to see them because we'll live closer."

 

4. Be prepared to have multiple conversations. The person may not show a strong reaction right away. They will need time to process and think about it. They may come back to you with questions later.

 

5. Try using visual supports. Supply visual schedules and social stories as needed. Help them understand what is happening, and what will happen next.

  • Check the library for books about moving house.

 

6. Keep them updated. Mark things clearly on the calendar and make sure it's somewhere easy to see.[3] Notify them of any important changes.

  • Some people love hearing the details of how everything works. If so, you can share information all about how buying and selling a house works.

 

7. Show them pictures of the new place. If you have photos of the new house, show them to your loved one. You can also show photos of the yard and the neighborhood. A teen or adult might like to see a map.

 

8. Let them see each room emptied. Coming home to an empty room can be startling. Try having your family member help sweep each room once it's empty, to help them understand that things have been moved out.[4]

  • If it's going to happen while they're gone (such as during school or work), tell them about it. Say something like "Today I am going to empty the living room, so when you come home, it will have no furniture. You can run around there if you want."

 

9. Check in on how they're feeling. Autistic people may not always tell you their emotions because they may not know how or they may not understand that you want to know. Asking questions may encourage them to open up.

  • "What do you think about the move so far?"
  • "Do you have any worries about moving to London?"
  • "What's on your mind today?"

 

10. Make a promise about something fun that can happen after the move. Having something to look forward to can help your loved one cope with the adjustment. Think about what your family member enjoys and plan a special treat. (Make sure that it's a promise you can keep.)

  • "On the weekend after we move, we are going to go get ice cream."
  • "When we go to our new house, there will be a box inside with a new toy for you."
  • "When we move in, your brother has promised to drive you to the library so you can check out lots of books about [special interest]."
  • "There is a beautiful park close to our new house. We can take walks there together as a family."

 

Method 2 - Making Physical Preparations

1. Photograph the house and yard before you start changing anything. Take good photos of every room. Photograph the yard and your family member's favorite outdoor hangouts if you have time. You might have your loved one help, or you may do it yourself. This way, if they want to talk about the old house, you'll have photos to show them.

  • Some autistic people miss their old homes very much, especially if they lived there for a long time. Having photos can be comforting.

 

2. Keep their bedroom the same for as long as possible. Save bedrooms until the end. That way, the bedroom can stay fairly consistent for most of the time.

 

3. Ask your loved one if they want to help pack. Some people might enjoy helping pack up their things. Others might find it to be a source of anxiety, and they might prefer that you pack up alone.

 

4. Pack and label a box of essentials that you can access quickly once you reach your new house. In addition to boxes of basic toiletries and meal prep items, you'll need some essentials for your autistic loved one.

  • A few days' worth of clothes, including a sweater
  • Medication for stomachaches, headaches, and other possible issues

 

5. Have your family member help you pack a backpack of treasured and necessary objects. Think about what helps them feel better when they're stressed and put some of these items inside. This bag can stay with your loved one the whole time and help ensure that they're as comfortable as possible.

  • Favorite electronics
  • Comfort objects
  • Headphones and/or earplugs
  • Fidget toys/tools
  • Medication (including as-needed medication)
  • A few snacks

 

Method 3 - Choosing a Home

Environment can play a role in your loved one's stress levels and success adjusting.

1. Consider the quality of local schools for a child, especially special education services. See if you can find out what students and parents think about the school.

  • See if the special education focuses on empowerment or control. Some schools focus more on controlling behavior than teaching useful skills.

 

2. Look for a quiet neighborhood. Low-pitched noises from airplanes, cars, and motorcycles can prevent an autistic person from focusing and socializing. Try to avoid living near a busy street or airport.

  • Low-traffic areas can be easier for driving and biking, which could support your loved one's independence skills now or later on. It's especially important if the person doesn't understand traffic safety.

 

3. Think about how quiet the house is. Loud air conditioning and heating can be disruptive for autistics who are sensitive to sound.[5] When visiting, take a moment of silence to listen to the sounds of the house.

  • Keep in mind that rock and metal surfaces, such as granite countertops, tend to be noisier when something bumps them.

 

4. Consider the person's ability to "hide" in the house. Many autistic people like to be able to hide away somewhere quiet when overwhelmed. A home with well-defined spaces or a few "hidey holes" may be more attractive to an autistic person.

  • Open-concept houses are usually a bad idea for autistic people. These can be noisy and make the person feel too exposed.
  • Let them have their own room, if possible.
  • Consider if there's a special place the person could hide when they want to be alone, like in their closet or under the stairs. Having a hiding place can reduce the risk of running off, because the person already has a safe place to go.

 

5. Keep in mind that every autistic person is different. Different people enjoy different things. Think about what your loved one likes and use that information to help you make decisions about the new home.

 

Method 4 - Handling Moving Day

1. Consider having the person away during the moving time. Moving day is stressful for everyone, especially autistic people. See if they can be out of the house with a responsible family member or babysitter. Tell them that they'll go to the new home afterwards.[6]

  • It's not uncommon for autistic people to notice and absorb stress from others around them. Keeping them away can mitigate this.
  • It'll also be helpful for you to not need to worry about them while you focus on moving.

 

2. Try assigning the person a basic task to occupy them. If you can't keep them out of the house, distract them with an easy chore. Try letting them sweep, use a feather duster, check that everything got packed, keep an eye on a child, or do some other chore.

  • Make sure that the task isn't too challenging and that they know they can stop if they need a break. That way they can pause if they're over-stressed.
  • Don't place too many demands on them. Remember, they're going to be stressed.

 

3. Keep their safety in mind. If they likes to climb things or run off, there could be a safety hazard. Consider the height of box piles and the open doors and windows.

  • If your loved one runs off when upset, assume that moving might make them upset. See if there's a quiet place indoors to designate as somewhere they can safely "run away" to.

 

4. Label your emotions in front of the person. Autistic people can be emotional sponges, absorbing all the stress that they see from others around them. You can reduce stress by telling your loved one how you feel and why.

  • "I'm stressed because this is so much work. You can help me by going to your room and playing on your tablet so I can focus."
  • "I'm frustrated because we are having problems with the move. It's not your fault."
  • "I'm feeling overwhelmed. I need to be alone."
  • "I'm sorry I raised my voice. I didn't sleep well and it's hard for me to think straight. Normally I know better, but today I forgot."

 

5. Unpack their room first. Try to arrange their furniture similarly to how it was at the old place.[7] Unpack any comfort items and beloved objects that could calm your family member.

  • Your loved one may hide in their new closet. This is normal and no cause for concern; they're likely overwhelmed. Let them hide in there for as long as they need to. They may enjoy having a tablet and/or comfort item while they are hiding.

 

Method 5 - Helping with Adjustment

1. Be prepared for a delayed reaction. While some autistic people show immediate signs of stress, others seem okay for a week or two and then show their distress.[8]

  • Encouraging them to talk about their feelings may help reduce bottled-up emotions.

 

2. Don't be surprised if the person hates the new house at first. The house is different, and changes can be hard for autistic people. Accept their feelings while quietly keeping in mind that your loved one will adjust in time. Here are some things you can say if they hate the house:

  • "I know you miss our old home. It's okay to be sad about it."
  • "Change is hard. You don't have to like it."
  • "This house is not perfect. You don't have to love every part of it."
  • "I know this isn't easy for me. It's okay to tell me how you feel."

 

3. Expect higher levels of stress. The person may experience more frequent meltdowns/shutdowns, and they may even regress for a time. This can be hard for both of you, since you're also dealing with extra stress. Recognize that your loved one is doing their best, just like you are.

  • Sometimes, subtle things can be stressful, like the fact that the light switches are different.[9]

 

4. Take time to hang out with them and show how much you love them. Your family member needs you even more in times of stress. By showing your love and taking some time to have fun together, you can help them feel better about this change.

  • Offer hugs, kisses, and snuggles (if the person likes them)
  • Involve them in fun activities
  • Play games that your loved one enjoys
  • Ask questions about their special interest
  • Surprise them with a small gift

 

5. Remember to take time for yourself. Moving house is hard on everyone, so be sure to take time for your feelings.

  • You can't be a good helper if you're exhausted. Make time for sleeping, eating well, relaxing, and doing other types of self care. Your calmness will help your loved one.
  • Don't feel guilty for setting them up in front of a tablet or TV screen. They get to relax and you get to focus on something else for a while. (Besides, many autistics learn social skills from TV.)
